Unity Prototype for Trust Calibration in Highly Automated Driving

A Unity-based prototype and study design exploring how human-in-the-loop user-interface optimization could support trust calibration in highly automated driving. The work focused on translating research questions into reliability scenarios, visualization concepts, and an experimental structure before the study direction was later revised.

Research Motivation

Appropriate trust in automated vehicles means user trust should match system reliability. Overtrust and undertrust can both be problematic, so the prototype focused on how interface cues might support more appropriate trust calibration.

Research Questions

The presentation asked how human-in-the-loop optimization of automated-vehicle visualizations might influence trust calibration across reliability levels, and how optimized visualization, static visualization, and no visualization could differ with respect to trust calibration, cognitive load, and visual appeal.

Experimental Concept

The planned 3 x 3 design combined optimized visualization, static visualization, and no visualization with low, medium, and high system reliability. Trust, cognitive load, and visual appeal were the intended dependent variables.

My Contribution

My contribution focused on translating the study concept into a Unity-based prototype. I worked on the experimental structure, reliability-scenario implementation, visualization concepts, and technical preparation for a planned study comparing optimized, static, and no-visualization conditions across different reliability levels.

Study Design Concept

The planned study compared three visualization approaches and three reliability levels. The goal was to investigate whether adaptive, human-in-the-loop UI optimization could help users calibrate trust more appropriately than static visualization or no visualization.

This version remained a prototype/study-design implementation and was not completed as a participant study. The work therefore centered on shaping the experimental structure, reliability scenarios, and visualization ideas in Unity rather than on collecting empirical results.

Visualization Approach

Optimized visualization, static visualization, and no visualization were defined as the three planned interface conditions.

System Reliability

Low, medium, and high reliability levels were planned so trust could be examined against different qualities of automated-vehicle behavior.

Dependent Variables

Trust, cognitive load, and visual appeal were identified as the intended measures for comparing the visualization approaches.

Unity Scenario Implementation

The Unity prototype represented reliability differences through scenario events and visualization manipulations. Reliability situations included pedestrian crossings, false positives, false negatives, covered areas, trajectory cues, and segmentation-based highlighting.

Reliability Levels

Low, medium, and high reliability conditions were planned as separate scenario variants so trust could be examined against changing system behavior.

Pedestrian Crossing Events

Pedestrian-crossing situations provided a concrete scene for expressing reliability cues and for comparing how different visualization approaches might frame the event.

Detection Edge Cases

False-positive and false-negative examples were illustrated through occlusion and covered-area concepts to communicate uncertainty, hidden road users, or incorrect detections.

Scene-Based Visual Feedback

Trajectory cues and segmentation-based highlighting were explored as ways of showing expected movement and reliability-dependent visual emphasis inside the driving scene.

Reliability in Visualization

The prototype explored several visualization concepts for communicating reliability-related information inside the driving scene, including pedestrian cues, occlusion highlighting, covered areas, trajectory cues, and segmentation-based highlighting.

Pedestrian Icon

Pedestrian-related cues were used to explore how visual feedback could support awareness of crossing events under different reliability levels.

Occlusion Highlighting

Occlusion highlighting was used to represent cases where relevant road users may be hidden, missed, or incorrectly detected.

Covered Areas

Covered-area visualization explored how the system could communicate which parts of the environment were covered by perception and which areas may remain uncertain.

Trajectories

Trajectory cues were explored as a way to communicate expected movement paths and help users interpret upcoming behavior in the automated-driving scene.

Segmentation Highlighting

Segmentation highlighting explored object-level visual feedback with reliability-dependent application rates.

Prototype Status

This project reached the technical prototype and study-design stage. The planned participant study was not conducted in this version. Later, the research direction was revised and developed into a different ongoing study platform. This page therefore focuses on the implementation process, scenario design, and visualization prototyping rather than final empirical results.

What this project demonstrates

  • Translating HVI research questions into a Unity prototype.
  • Designing experimental conditions for trust calibration.
  • Implementing reliability scenarios in an automated-driving simulation.
  • Exploring visual feedback concepts for automated-vehicle behavior.
  • Preparing a technical foundation for later study development.
  • Iterating from an early prototype toward a more refined research platform.
